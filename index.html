荣耀 70 & 平板 8 营销方案

荣耀创意挑战赛 · 全国第五

下方为比赛完整 PPT 方案，共 20 页

大二期间，我作为组长与一位队友参与了2022年荣耀创意精英挑战赛。复赛阶段，我们围绕荣耀 70 的「主角模式」，以毕业季为背景拍摄了一支叙事型短片；在决赛阶段，我们为荣耀 70 与荣耀平板 8 面向大学生群体设计整合营销方案，并最终获得全国第五名。

在这份方案中，我当时的核心思路是：基于大学生群体“重体验、强表达、偏感性消费”的特征，将产品的多屏协同与大屏体验转译为学习、创作与娱乐等具体使用场景，并以“多元宇宙”作为创意母题，串联 TVC 传播、KOL 投放与校园线下展览，试图通过情绪共鸣与参与机制提升品牌好感度与曝光。

但从现在回看，这个方案在策略层面存在较为系统性的不足：

用“流行概念”替代“真实需求”

当时选择“元宇宙”作为核心创意，本质是借势热点而非从用户出发。问题在于，这一概念与“为什么要买一台荣耀设备”之间缺乏直接因果关系，用户很难将抄象叙事转化为具体购买理由。换句话说，这是一个“好讲述但不好卖货”的创意。

用户洞察停留在“标签化”，缺乏决策驱动因素

我们提炼了如“感性消费”“追求体验”等特征，但这些更多是对群体的描述，而非影响决策的关键变量。方案中缺失的是：用户在什么情境下会犹豫？在什么条件下会下单？例如“效率 vs 娱乐”“价格 vs 性能”的权衡并未被纳入策略核心。

产品表达缺乏“非你不可”的理由

虽然完成了从功能到场景的映射，但这些场景并未建立与荣耀产品的强绑定关系，本质上是“这个场景可以用荣耀”，而不是“这个场景更适合用荣耀”。缺乏差异化论证，使产品在决策链路中容易被替代。

传播策略本质仍是“品牌单向输出”

当时采用 TVC+KOL+线下展的组合，在学生竞赛语境中是完整的，但本质仍属于中心化传播逻辑：品牌生产内容，用户被动接收。缺少用户自发参与与内容再生产机制，导致传播难以形成持续扩散。

转化路径缺失，结果指标缺乏支撑逻辑

方案中直接给出了曝光、转化率与销量预期，但没有拆解从“看到内容”到“完成购买”的具体路径，也没有解释每一环节如何影响转化。这在真实商业环境中会被视为关键缺口，因为 ROI 无法被验证或复用。

数据设计偏“展示性”，缺乏可执行性

指标设置更接近结果陈述，而非运营过程中的监测工具，例如缺少点击率、加购率、转化成本等中间指标，也没有对应的数据采集与归因方式。

如果将这一项目放到2026年重新执行，我会进行以下结构性调整：

01

从“概念驱动”转向“问题驱动”——以“多设备切换带来的效率损耗”为核心切口，将多屏协同从“功能卖点”升级为“解决方案”，直接嵌入用户日常学习与生活决策场景。

02

重构内容逻辑：从品牌叙事转向真实记录——放弃 TVC 作为核心内容形态，转而以真实用户的多设备使用场景为内容主体，通过可感知的使用体验建立信任。

03

从 KOL 投放转向 KOC/UGC 驱动的内容生态——通过大量普通用户内容而非头部达人，构建更高真实性的种草环境，使“他人经验”成为主要说服力来源。

04

补全完整转化链路并建立数据闭环——明确“内容触达 → 兴趣产生 → 跳转电商 → 下单转化”的路径，并设置可量化指标（CTR、加购率、转化率等），使每一阶段都可被优化与复盘。

05

强化产品差异表达，建立选择理由——将“多屏协同”的具体优势量化并可视化，让用户理解“为什么这套组合优于替代方案”。

这个项目让我第一次意识到，营销方案的价値不在于“讲了一个完整的故事”，而在于是否能够解释清楚：用户为什么会因此改变决策。这种从“表达导向”转向“决策导向”的转变，也成为我后续所有项目中持续修正的核心。

During my sophomore year, I led a team with one teammate to participate in the 2022 Honor Creative Elite Challenge. In the semi-final round, we produced a narrative short film centered on the "Protagonist Mode" of the Honor 70, using the graduation season as the backdrop. In the final round, we designed an integrated marketing campaign targeting university students for the Honor 70 and Honor Pad 8, ultimately achieving fifth place nationwide.

In this campaign, my core approach was: based on the characteristics of university students—valuing experience, strong self-expression, and a tendency toward emotional consumption—we translated the product’s multi-screen collaboration and large-screen experience into concrete usage scenarios such as studying, creating, and entertainment. Using the concept of a "multiverse" as the central creative theme, we connected TVC communication, KOL placements, and offline campus exhibitions, aiming to enhance brand favorability and exposure through emotional resonance and participatory mechanisms.

However, in retrospect, the campaign revealed several systematic shortcomings at the strategic level:

Substituting "trendy concepts" for "real needs"

Choosing the "metaverse" as the core creative concept was essentially an attempt to leverage a popular trend rather than starting from user needs. The concept lacked a direct causal link to "why one should purchase an Honor device." Users found it difficult to translate abstract narratives into concrete purchase motivations—it was "easy to tell but hard to sell."

User insights remained at the level of "labeling," lacking decision-driving factors

We identified traits such as "emotional consumption" and "experience-oriented behavior," but these were descriptive rather than explanatory of decision-making. Missing was a deeper understanding of when users hesitate and under what conditions they decide to purchase—trade-offs like "efficiency vs. entertainment" and "price vs. performance" were not incorporated into the strategic core.

Product communication lacked a compelling "reason-to-choose"

Although we mapped product functions to scenarios (studying, fitness, photo editing), these scenarios did not establish a strong, exclusive link to Honor products. The message was "this scenario can be done with Honor," rather than "this scenario is better with Honor." The lack of differentiated justification made the product easily substitutable.

The communication strategy remained fundamentally "one-way brand output"

While the combination of TVC, KOLs, and offline exhibitions appeared comprehensive within a student competition context, it still followed a centralized communication logic: the brand produced content, and users passively received it. There was little mechanism for user-generated participation or content reproduction, making it difficult to sustain organic diffusion.

Missing conversion pathways and unsupported outcome metrics

The proposal directly presented expectations for exposure, conversion rates, and sales, but failed to break down the actual path from "content exposure" to "purchase completion," or explain how each stage contributed to conversion. In a real business context, this would be considered a critical gap, as ROI could neither be validated nor replicated.

Data design was presentation-oriented rather than actionable

The metrics resembled outcome statements rather than operational monitoring tools. There was a lack of intermediate indicators such as click-through rate, add-to-cart rate, and cost per conversion, as well as corresponding methods for data collection and attribution.

If I were to re-execute this project in 2026, I would make the following structural adjustments:

01

From "concept-driven" to "problem-driven" strategy—Focus on the inefficiencies caused by switching between devices as the key entry point, upgrading multi-screen collaboration from a "feature" into a "solution" embedded directly into users' everyday study and life decisions.

02

Reconstructing content logic: from brand storytelling to real-life documentation—Instead of using TVCs as the core content format, center content on real user scenarios of multi-device usage, building trust through tangible experiences rather than relying on emotional narratives.

03

From KOL-driven exposure to a KOC/UGC-driven content ecosystem—By leveraging a large volume of ordinary user-generated content rather than top influencers, create a more authentic environment for product discovery, where peer experiences become the primary source of persuasion.

04

Completing the conversion funnel and establishing a data loop—Clearly define the path from "content exposure → interest generation → e-commerce redirection → purchase conversion," and assign measurable metrics (CTR, add-to-cart rate, conversion rate, etc.) to each stage.

05

Strengthening product differentiation and building a clear "reason to choose"—Quantify and visualize the specific advantages of multi-screen collaboration, helping users understand why this ecosystem outperforms alternatives.

This project was the first time I realized that the value of a marketing proposal does not lie in telling a complete story, but in clearly explaining why it can change user decisions. This shift—from an "expression-oriented" approach to a "decision-oriented" one—has since become a core principle that I continuously refine in my subsequent projects.

"此间：八仙非遗"沉浸式街区

在大三期间，我与团队参与全国大学生艺术项目策划大赛，选择"城市更新"赛道，围绕山东烟台蓬莱戚府里街区的改造进行策划，并最终获得全国二等奖。

项目以当地"八仙过海"传说为核心文化IP，结合戚继光故居的历史背景，尝试通过"神话+非遗+商业"的方式激活衰落街区。我们以八仙各自的法器为灵感，对街区原有建筑与业态进行重构，设计了包括主题店铺、非遗体验空间、NPC互动机制在内的商业体系，并通过花车巡游、沉浸式戏剧演出与无人机灯光秀，打造一个兼具观演与参与体验的"仙境式"文旅场景。

从当时的创作逻辑来看，我们的核心思路是：以强文化IP（八仙）作为叙事主线，将"看"（演出）、"逛"（街区）、"玩"（互动任务）、"买"（商业消费）串联成一个完整体验路径，试图通过沉浸式叙事提升游客停留时间与消费转化。

但从现在的视角复盘，这个项目在"创意完整性"之外，存在几个更本质的问题：

IP使用停留在"视觉与主题层"，缺乏内容持续生产能力

我们将八仙拆解为店铺、NPC与演出元素（如巡游表演与集章机制），但本质上仍是"主题包装"，缺乏持续更新的内容机制（如剧情迭代、角色成长、节庆变化）。这意味着项目初期有吸引力，但长期复访动力不足。

体验设计偏"堆叠"，缺乏核心动线与记忆点聚焦

方案中包含演出、巡游、店铺、DIY、点灯仪式等多种模块，但没有明确的"核心爆点体验"。用户路径更像"内容集合"，而不是围绕一个强体验展开的叙事动线，容易导致体验分散、记忆不集中。

商业模型不清晰，消费与成本结构未闭环

虽然设计了多种店铺（换装、香囊、酒馆、茶饮等）及价格体系，但没有回答关键问题：哪些是引流型业态，哪些是利润核心？客单价与客流量如何支撑整体回本周期？演出、NPC、无人机等高成本项目如何被摊销？这也是当时评委指出"资金问题"的本质原因。

重内容轻运营，对现实落地复杂度预估不足

项目涉及非遗传承人、演员训练、花车制作、政府审批、交通管制等多方协同，但我们更多从"理想体验"出发，而非"运营难度与组织成本"出发，低估了实际执行门槛。

目标人群过于宽泛，缺乏主力消费人群聚焦

方案同时覆盖家庭客群、Z世代与周边游客，但不同人群的消费动机与行为差异较大，缺乏明确的"第一优先用户"，导致产品设计在深度上被稀释。

传播策略偏传统，缺乏"自传播内容机制"设计

虽然有短视频、打卡与达人探店等推广方式，但更多是"投放思维"，而非在产品层面设计天然可传播的内容（如强视觉奇观、冲突性互动或可复制的UGC模板）。

如果从2026年的视角重新优化这个项目，我会进行以下关键调整：

01

从"IP包装"转向"IP运营"——不仅做八仙主题视觉，而是构建持续更新的内容系统（如角色剧情、节庆版本、任务系统），让街区具备"持续可玩性"。

02

聚焦单一核心体验，打造超级爆点——例如将"无人机+八仙斗法"打造成夜间唯一核心IP事件，而不是分散在多个中等体验中，提高传播效率与记忆强度。

03

重构商业模型，明确盈利结构——区分引流业态（演出/打卡）与盈利业态（餐饮/体验/文创），并反推客流与定价，确保模型在财务上成立。

04

从"设计项目"转向"设计运营系统"——包括人员培训、内容更新节奏、淡旺季策略等，使项目具备长期运行能力，而非一次性方案。

05

以"内容可传播性"反推产品设计——在项目初期就考虑"什么内容会被拍、被转发"，例如强化视觉奇观、角色互动或参与挑战，让传播成为产品的一部分，而非后置动作。

这个项目让我第一次接触到"文化+商业+空间"的复杂系统，也让我意识到：一个成功的文旅项目，不只是"好看和好玩"，更重要的是能否在长期运营中成立。这种从"创意策划"走向"商业与运营思维"的转变，对我后续的项目产生了很大影响。

In my junior year, I participated in a national-level art and project planning competition in China, focusing on urban regeneration. Together with my team, we developed a proposal to revitalize a declining historic district (Qifu Li) in Penglai, a coastal city in Shandong Province, and won the National Second Prize.

The project was built around the local cultural legend of the "Eight Immortals Crossing the Sea" — a well-known Chinese myth in which eight legendary figures each use their unique magical objects to traverse the ocean — combined with the historical site of General Qi Jiguang's former residence (a famous Ming Dynasty military leader known for defending China's eastern coast). We aimed to reactivate the underdeveloped district by integrating mythology, intangible cultural heritage, and commercial experiences.

Our approach was to transform the existing street into an immersive cultural-commercial space: each building and store was redesigned based on one of the Eight Immortals and their symbolic artifacts, accompanied by interactive retail, craft-based experiences, live performances, and nighttime spectacles such as parades and drone light shows.

From a strategic perspective, our core idea was to use a strong cultural IP as a narrative backbone, connecting "watching" (performances), "exploring" (street experience), "interacting" (tasks and NPCs), and "consuming" (retail and F&B), in order to increase visitor engagement time and spending.

However, looking back from today's perspective, the project also revealed several structural limitations:

IP usage remained at a thematic level, lacking long-term content sustainability

While we translated the Eight Immortals into stores, characters, and performances (e.g., parades and collectible tasks), the IP functioned more as a visual and thematic layer rather than a continuously evolving content system. This limits repeat visits and long-term engagement.

Experience design was additive rather than focused

The project combined multiple elements — performances, parades, retail, DIY workshops, rituals — but lacked a single, defining "anchor experience." As a result, the user journey risked becoming fragmented rather than memorable.

Business model lacked clarity and financial feasibility

Although we designed various retail formats and pricing structures, we did not clearly define revenue drivers, cost structure, or payback logic. In particular, high-cost elements such as live performances and drone shows were not sufficiently tied to sustainable income streams — a concern also raised by judges during the competition.

Operational complexity was underestimated

The proposal involved coordination across multiple stakeholders, including local government, performers, intangible heritage artisans, and infrastructure management. However, our planning focused more on the ideal user experience than on execution feasibility and operational scalability.

Target audience was too broad and insufficiently prioritized

We attempted to appeal to families, Gen Z visitors, and regional tourists simultaneously, without clearly defining a primary revenue-driving audience, which diluted the depth of product design.

Marketing relied on distribution rather than built-in shareability

While we proposed social media promotion and influencer visits, we did not fully embed "shareable moments" into the product itself, limiting organic content generation.

If I were to revisit this project from a 2026 perspective, I would focus on several key improvements:

01

Shift from IP theming to IP operations, building evolving storylines, seasonal events, and character-driven interactions;

02

Define a single flagship experience (e.g., a signature nighttime show) to concentrate attention and improve memorability;

03

Reconstruct the business model, clearly separating traffic-driving elements from profit-generating ones;

04

Design for long-term operations, including content updates, staffing systems, and seasonal strategies;

05

Integrate content virality into product design, ensuring that the experience naturally generates shareable moments.

This project was my first attempt at working with a complex system combining culture, space, and commerce. It also marked a shift in my thinking — from focusing on creative concepts to understanding how ideas translate into sustainable business and operational models.
